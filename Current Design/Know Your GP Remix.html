Know Your GP

Hi, I’m Paul Finney.

I’ve spent twelve years in the trenches of Indian startups — as a builder, a creative director, a founder, an angel investor, and a media operator. This is what brought me to Imagine Fund.

The Letter

I moved to Bangalore in 2012 as a ninety-day-old fresher. No network, no money, no playbook. My first real job was as the first employee of Campus Diaries — a youth expression platform backed by TV Mohandas Pai through Aarin Capital and later 3one4 Capital.1 I stayed four years. We built community properties across hundreds of universities — film festivals, spoken word stages, TEDx partnerships, scaled programs reaching thousands of students. That experience taught me something I still carry: attention is infrastructure, and community is the only moat that survives a cycle.

Even before graduating with my Computer Science degree from Chandigarh Engineering College, I was already chasing creative work on the side. I shot photographs at Wills Lifestyle India Fashion Week, toured with musicians as their official photographer, covered India Golf Tour, India Bike Week.2 The camera was my first tool for understanding how stories move through people. It still is.

After college, I wanted to put creativity and technology together. I moved into product management at a time when AI was still an acronym most people couldn’t define. At IceCream Labs, I became the youngest person in the room pitching AI solutions to Shell’s Alicia Chang, working alongside the Pro CTO on enterprise deployments, and shipping Walmart’s first batch of AI use cases live in 2016–17.3

I then joined ParallelDots in Delhi — a machine intelligence group building everything from medical AI to internet-scale sentiment analysis. There, I launched an app called Carnifier AI, based on one of Andrej Karpathy’s early papers on predicting photo popularity. The app went viral. Ryan Hoover himself featured the launch.4 Consumer instinct was something I didn’t learn from a book. I learned it by shipping things that people actually used.

The deeper I went into AI, the more I wanted to build something of my own. I co-founded Eder Labs — a deeptech venture in AI and privacy, building trusted execution environments for machine learning workloads. We raised from Accel. We went through Cisco LaunchPad, Target Accelerator, NetApp Accelerator, and the Pioneers Batch 1 program. I was part of YC Startup School.5 That experience was humbling. I learned that I wasn’t cut out to be a deep infrastructure founder in the traditional sense — but I understood the builder’s journey intimately, because I had lived it from the inside.

I took an early exit from Eder Labs to start SpaceKayak, channeling everything I’d learned into a creative practice built for technically ambitious founders. The thesis was simple: the best companies in the world don’t just build great products — they build great narratives. SpaceKayak became the creative arm for companies like Emergent, Composio, Sentient AI, Nerve AI (now acquired by OpenAI), Sarvam AI, and IAIRO. In Europe, we became the creative partner for Vanagon Ventures and Flog Ventures. We shaped the brand for BFI — Vitalik Buterin and Sandeep Nailwal’s blockchain-for-public-health initiative.6

Along the way, my love for consumer experience led me to build Fireside — essentially India’s Clubhouse. The product was acquired by Chingari, and the platform scaled to over a hundred million downloads.7 I also did private consulting with Kaliani Group and Cogknit Semantics on battery intelligence and government-grade design projects. I became a two-time Awwwards recognised designer.

With my exit capital from Eder Labs, I started angel investing. Some bets worked, some didn’t survive the market cycle, but the founder conviction was always valid. Notable investments include Leap Wallet (backed by Pantera and CoinFund), Obvious Money (acquired by CoinDCX), Kayal (on track for potential IPO), and NextMind — doing breakthrough research in brain-computer interfaces.8

Media became another throughline. I co-hosted The Ramp Show with Suhas Motwani — two seasons, twenty-four episodes with some of the sharpest builders in the Indian crypto ecosystem. I ran This Week in Web3, a newsletter delivered for a hundred consecutive weeks to close to forty thousand readers, produced by SpaceKayak and powered by The Product House.9

I was the creative director for the largest ETH India conference — three thousand people in Bangalore in 2022. I’ve given guest lectures at Columbia University on privacy and AI. I’ve spoken at FIL Conference on the future of the composable internet. I was one of the first Indians selected for Balaji Srinivasan’s 1729 School (now The Network State). I completed the On Deck Design Fellowship.10

All of this — the building, the breaking, the shipping, the investing, the storytelling — led to one conviction: the earliest stage is where the highest-leverage work happens. Before startups exist. Before anyone else would bet.

That’s why I built Sanctuary. That’s why I’m raising Imagine Fund. Not because I read about this gap in a report. Because I’ve lived inside it for twelve years.
